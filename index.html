Life & Character

Biography

Prof. Dr. Ahmed Medhat Shams El Din was born in Cairo in 1931. He was the youngest of six siblings — four brothers and two sisters. His father was a distinguished educational figure who wrote and developed the high-school science curriculum in the early years of the twentieth century. Sadly, his father passed away when Shams El Din was just ten years old. Yet he was outlived by a remarkable wife who, coming from the Egyptian upper middle class, put all her effort, wealth, and wisdom into raising successful and independent children.

While Shams El Din grew up to become one of the foremost corrosion scientists of his generation, his brothers were equally accomplished in different fields: one a celebrated architect, one a pioneering computer engineer, and one a successful industrialist.

He completed his school years with distinction and was admitted to the Faculty of Science at Cairo University — at a time when Science, Engineering, Medicine, and Pharmacy were the most prestigious faculties in Egypt. As a student he did not yet know the depth of his passion for chemistry, but it became unmistakable when he began attending lectures given by the most notable scientists of his time. He was consistently the top of his class.

After graduating with his Bachelor of Science, he was appointed Teaching Assistant at the Faculty of Science, Cairo University, where he began his doctoral research while teaching undergraduate students. During his first year of teaching, he met the woman who would become his lifelong companion, wife, and the mother of his three daughters.

He pursued his doctoral work with characteristic rigour, earning his Ph.D. from Cairo University in 1956, then crossing to Europe to earn a second doctorate — Dr. Sc. Techn. — from the prestigious Zurich Federal Institute of Technology (ETH Zürich) in 1960. A third doctorate followed from Cairo University in 1964. Over a career spanning more than five decades, he produced hundreds of research papers published in leading international journals, contributed Arabic-language technical articles to engineering publications across the Arab world, and authored books on metal corrosion that are still referenced today.

His field — electrochemical corrosion science — governs the integrity of pipelines, desalination plants, bridges, ships, and the entire built infrastructure of modern civilisation. He dedicated his career to understanding, measuring, and combating the invisible forces that cause metals to decay. His research on the corrosion behaviour of copper alloys, stainless steels, titanium, and aluminium in Arabian Gulf seawater had direct impact on the design of desalination plants across the Gulf region.

He passed away in Cairo in 2008, leaving behind a towering body of scientific work, a family whose lives he shaped by example, and the memory of a man who made the most of every moment he was given.
